Problem Statement

The quality of education in Kenya is severely undermined by a critical lack of transparency and accountability in the disbursement of public funds to primary schools. Due to poor governance and corruption, financial resources intended for tuition, infrastructure, and accommodation are frequently misappropriated or inefficiently allocated. This leads directly to under-resourced and unsafe classrooms, a severe lack of learning materials, and inadequate support for teachers. Consequently, this failure perpetuates a cycle of educational inequality, disproportionately harming children in poor and rural communities.

Mission

We believe that by creating a transparent, public website to track fund disbursement to primary schools in Kenya, we can reduce corruption and ensure more money reaches its intended purpose — improving educational resources for all Kenyan children.

Funding Challenges

The government's core funding is not allocated to a list of specific schools, but rather is distributed per student to every single public secondary school in Nairobi. According to google, specific school by school disbursement data is treated as internal budgetary information by the Ministry of Education and is not typically released to the public. The core rule for public funding is very simple: every public secondary school in Nairobi gets a fixed amount of money for every student it enrolls. This system is called the Per-Student Capitation method.
The official rate the government promises to pay for each learner annually is Ksh 22,244. In practice, a school's total government funding is just its total number of students multiplied by that rate. This money is intended to cover all tuition fees and the school's basic running costs, like electricity, water, and maintenance.
Misallocation & Fraud • KSh 16.6 billion sent to 14 non-existent ("ghost") schools
• KSh 3.7 billion in overpayments due to "ghost students" / inflated data
The key challenge is that the government often does not disburse the full Ksh 22,244 due to national budget issues. Therefore, while that is the legal formula, the actual amount a school receives often ends up being much less than what is needed to cover costs.
